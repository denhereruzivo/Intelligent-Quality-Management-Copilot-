## 3. Discussion of proposal scope

This proposal responds to the AI4I Participant Self-Assessment Checklist and aligns with the National Quality Policy, Vision 2030, National AI Strategy, and AI4I Development Track.

### 3.1 Problem statement

Vision 2030 seeks to establish an upper-middle-income economy through industrialisation, innovation, productivity, and export-led growth. The Zimbabwe National Quality Policy (2026–2030) similarly seeks a harmonised National Quality Infrastructure (NQI) that supports industrialisation, trade competitiveness, consumer protection, environmental sustainability, and regional and global market integration.

These objectives require widespread use of internationally recognised Quality Management Systems (QMS), effective conformity assessment, and stronger accreditation of laboratories, inspection bodies, and certification bodies. Yet SMEs, local authorities, public institutions, and emerging industries face persistent barriers:

- Limited access to experienced ISO practitioners and consultants.
- High implementation costs and time-consuming preparation of procedures, policies, manuals, and records.
- Difficulty interpreting ISO standards and regulatory requirements.
- Inconsistent audit reporting, nonconformity management, root-cause analysis, and continual improvement.
- Language barriers and fragmented knowledge across standards, regulations, and guidance.

Denhe reRuzivo AI addresses these gaps through an AI-assisted platform combining LLMs, RAG, authorised ISO knowledge bases, and intelligent automation. The proposed workflows will help organisations develop reviewed procedures, policies, manuals, risk-and-opportunity registers, audit programmes, checklists, reports, nonconformities, root-cause analyses, corrective actions, and local-language learning material. All outputs remain subject to qualified human review and approval.

### 3.2 Target users

The platform is intended for SMEs; manufacturers; mining, agricultural, and food-processing enterprises; healthcare institutions; government ministries, departments and agencies; local authorities; universities and technical colleges; laboratories; inspection and certification bodies; quality consultants; auditors; regulators; export-oriented organisations; start-ups; and innovation hubs.

It supports management-system work associated with ISO 9001, ISO 14001, ISO 45001, ISO 22000, ISO 27001, ISO 31000, ISO 19011, ISO/IEC 17025, and ISO 15189.

## 4. Strategic alignment to Zimbabwe's national priorities

The platform supports the National Quality Policy by providing a unified quality-knowledge platform, making expertise more accessible, supporting standards harmonisation, improving accreditation readiness, and enabling a national quality culture through AI-assisted and multilingual learning. It can support consumer protection, environmental sustainability, AfCFTA, SADC, COMESA, WTO, and Agenda 2063 alignment while advancing Vision 2030 and the National AI Strategy.

## 5. Evidence that the problem matters

Zimbabwe's development agenda identifies industrialisation, innovation, productivity, export growth, standards, accreditation, and conformity assessment as critical enablers of competitiveness and consumer confidence. Certification and accreditation are practical indicators of a strong quality ecosystem.

### 5.1 Certification to international standards

The cited regional figures show that Zimbabwe has a strong base of certified organisations: 216 ISO 9001, 85 ISO 14001, 77 ISO 45001, and 23 ISO 22000 certifications. These figures exceed those of several regional comparators, including Mauritius, Angola, Namibia, Mozambique, and Zambia for relevant standards, but remain substantially below South Africa's scale.

This gap represents an opportunity to expand certification across manufacturing, mining, agriculture, healthcare, education, local authorities, and government institutions. Greater certification can improve export competitiveness, investor confidence, supply-chain access, product and service quality, environmental sustainability, occupational health and safety, and food-safety assurance.

AI can lower barriers by assisting with grounded document drafts, ISO-clause interpretation, audit planning, nonconformity recording, root-cause analysis, corrective-action planning, and continual-improvement workflows.

### 5.2 National impact: certification

The proposed solution supports Vision 2030, the National Quality Policy, the National AI Strategy, and the AI4I Challenge by improving governance, compliance, productivity, and export readiness. Increased adoption of management systems can help develop globally competitive businesses, higher-quality jobs, investment confidence, and trusted regional and international trade.

### 5.3 Accreditation of conformity-assessment bodies

According to SADCAS Accreditation Statistics dated 31 May 2026, Tanzania had 125 accredited facilities and Zimbabwe had 48, ahead of Namibia (37), Botswana (36), Zambia (28), Malawi (19), the Democratic Republic of Congo (17), Eswatini (15), and Mozambique (14). SADCAS had issued 442 accreditation certificates across 365 facilities in the SADC region.

Despite this foundation, organisations face high consultancy costs, limited access to practitioners, time-consuming documentation, inconsistent records, limited internal-auditing capacity, and poor understanding of ISO requirements. These constraints limit growth in accredited laboratories, inspection bodies, and certification bodies.

### 5.4 Learning from Tanzania

Tanzania's use of Swahili in business, government, and quality-management material illustrates the accessibility benefits of local-language content. English remains primary for ISO documentation in Zimbabwe, yet Shona and Ndebele can improve understanding during implementation, training, auditing, and operational control.

The proposed platform will support qualified review of translations and plain-language explanations, multilingual training material, audit checklists, and reviewed management-system documentation. This is a competitiveness capability, not merely a translation feature.

### 5.5 National impact: accreditation

Denhe reRuzivo AI can support increased management-system adoption, growth in accredited conformity-assessment bodies, improved operational efficiency, export readiness under AfCFTA, investor confidence, and wider access to quality-management knowledge. Combining AI with Zimbabwe's quality infrastructure can strengthen certification, accreditation, and the competitiveness of Zimbabwean products and services.
